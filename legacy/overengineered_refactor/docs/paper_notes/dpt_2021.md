# Paper Note: DPT (Ranftl et al., 2021)

## Basic info
- Title: Vision Transformers for Dense Prediction
- Authors: René Ranftl, Alexey Bochkovskiy, Vladlen Koltun
- Venue / Year: arXiv preprint, 2021
- Link: arXiv:2103.13413

## Problem setting
- Task: Dense prediction, with main experiments on monocular depth estimation and semantic segmentation.
- Supervision type: Supervised / mixed large-scale depth training for the monocular depth experiments.
- Relative or metric depth?: Mainly relative / affine-invariant during large-scale pretraining, then fine-tuned for dataset-specific depth benchmarks such as NYUv2 and KITTI.
- Main benchmark(s): MiDaS-style zero-shot cross-dataset depth evaluation, NYUv2, KITTI Eigen split; also ADE20K and Pascal Context for segmentation.

## Core idea
- One-sentence summary: Replace the usual CNN backbone in dense prediction with a ViT backbone, then reassemble intermediate transformer tokens into multi-scale image-like features for a convolutional decoder.
- Main technical idea: The transformer keeps a high-resolution token representation with global receptive field throughout the backbone, and the decoder reconstructs spatial feature maps from multiple transformer stages for fine-grained dense outputs.
- Why it should work: Dense prediction needs both global scene context and local detail, and transformers naturally provide global context earlier and more uniformly than CNNs that aggressively downsample.

## Method details
- Backbone / architecture:
  - Encoder-decoder architecture called DPT (Dense Prediction Transformer).
  - Backbone variants: ViT-Base, ViT-Large, and ViT-Hybrid.
  - Patch size: 16.
  - Tokens from multiple transformer stages are reassembled into image-like feature maps at different resolutions.
  - A RefineNet-style fusion decoder progressively fuses and upsamples these features.
- Losses:
  - For monocular depth: scale- and shift-invariant trimmed loss on inverse depth, plus a gradient-matching loss.
  - For segmentation: cross-entropy plus auxiliary loss.
- Special components:
  - Read/reassemble operation to convert transformer tokens back into spatial feature maps.
  - Multi-scale feature extraction from several transformer layers.
  - Global readout token is injected into dense features via a projection-based readout mechanism.
  - Handles variable image sizes by interpolating positional embeddings.
- Training data:
  - Large-scale depth training uses an extended MiDaS-style meta-dataset called MIX 6, with about 1.4M images.
  - Encoder initialized from ImageNet-pretrained weights.
  - For depth, images are resized so the longer side is 384, then random 384×384 crops are used.

## Results
- Main claims:
  - DPT substantially improves monocular depth estimation over the prior MiDaS-style fully convolutional baseline.
  - It also sets state of the art on NYUv2, KITTI, and ADE20K at the time.
- Representative depth results:
  - Zero-shot transfer (Table 1): DPT-Large and DPT-Hybrid both outperform MiDaS; the paper reports more than 28% average relative improvement over the original MiDaS for DPT-Large and more than 23% for DPT-Hybrid.
  - NYUv2 (Table 2): DPT-Hybrid achieves δ<1.25 = 0.904, Abs Rel = 0.110, RMSE = 0.357, log10 = 0.045.
  - KITTI Eigen split (Table 3): DPT-Hybrid achieves δ<1.25 = 0.959, Abs Rel = 0.062, RMSE = 2.573, RMSE log = 0.092.
- Strengths:
  - Very clear architectural argument for why transformers help dense prediction.
  - Strong empirical gains on both zero-shot transfer and benchmark fine-tuning.
  - Good evidence that gains are not only from more data: they retrain the MiDaS CNN baseline on the larger dataset and DPT still wins.
  - Produces more globally coherent and sharper depth maps qualitatively.
- Weaknesses / limitations:
  - Strongly benefits from large-scale training; this is part of why DPT works so well.
  - Heavier and more complex than older CNN baselines.
  - The paper is more about architecture than about loss innovations tailored to scale-invariant ranking metrics.
  - For a small course project, reproducing full pretraining from scratch is unrealistic.

## Relevance to our project
- Most relevant idea: A strong transformer-based dense prediction backbone can be an excellent baseline for monocular depth, especially when fine-tuned rather than trained from scratch.
- What seems reusable:
  - DPT-style transformer encoder-decoder design;
  - multi-scale feature fusion from transformer stages;
  - the general argument that global context plus fine detail helps depth quality.
- What probably does not transfer:
  - reproducing MIX 6 pretraining ourselves;
  - relying on scale/shift-invariant pretraining alone without adapting to our exact evaluation setup.
- Should this influence our baseline or twist?: Yes. This paper strongly supports using a DPT-like or transformer-based pretrained model family as a baseline reference. It is more useful as a baseline architecture choice than as a source of a small novel twist.

## Short takeaway
- DPT is a key paper because it shows that transformers can outperform fully convolutional backbones for dense prediction, including monocular depth.
- For our project, the biggest lesson is architectural: a pretrained transformer dense predictor is likely a much stronger starting point than older CNN baselines.
- We should treat DPT as an important baseline/reference family, while looking elsewhere for a lightweight project-specific twist.
